13.3. Surface, Interfaces and Assembly Analysis with PISA¶

PISA (Protein Interfaces, Surfaces and Assemblies) performs the calculation of macromolecular surfaces and interfaces and their various properties, and predicts probable macromolecular assemblies (complexes).

PISA delivers the following data:

  • crystallised entities (monomers, chains) and their contacts (interfaces)
  • surface and interface areas, residues and atoms
  • hydrogen bonds, salt bridges and disulphide bonds in macromolecular interfaces
  • free energy of protein folding
  • energy of hydrophobic interaction between macromolecules and hydrophobic effect of individual residues
  • multiparametric assessment of macromolecular affinity (interaction radar)
  • probable macromolecular assemblies, their surface and buried areas, free energy of dissociation, dissociation patterns, ralation to crystal interfaces and assemblage details
  • concentration profiles of assembly stock with stoichiometry defined by crystal content

On input, PISA requires only atomic coordinates of sufficiently well refined structures. Do not use PISA if your structure is not yet complete and ready for PDB deposition, because even a small imperfectness in crystal contacts may make the difference between estimating a particuar interface as binding or not.

PISA does not have any input parameters that should be manipulated by user.

Basic principles

PISA calculates macromolecular interfaces (crystal contacts) as surface patches that have lost contact with the solvent upon crystallisation. For each interface, PISA calculates the binding energy as a sum of contributions from the solvation energy, hydrogen bonds, salt bridges and entropy of immobilised structural features (such as side chains) on macromolecular surface.

The calculate interface binding energies are used for the calculation of free Gibbs energy of dissociation of all macromolacular assemblies that can exist in given crystal packing. Assemblies with positive free Gibbs energy have chances to exist in solution, and, therefore, are reported as probable oligomeric states (complexes). A stable oligomeric state has chances to be the Biological Unit, responsible for certain biochemical functions.

The stability of predicted oligomeric states is assessed on concentration scale (Stock analysis), which may be useful for identifying bogus and promising Biological Units.

Further reading

Full details of PISA method and important hints for the interpretation of PISA results are given in [Krissinel and Henrick, 2007]. Stock analysis, which is important for the assessment of macromolecular complexes, predicted by PISA, is described in [Krissinel 2015]. The agreement between oligomeric states inferred from crystal packings and observed in solutions is investigated in [Krissinel 2010].
